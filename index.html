Chapter One

Who I am (the honest version)

I think in systems and care about people, which is a useful combination when your job involves making complex things usable for real humans in real classrooms.

ch. 01 who I am

I'm a former K–4 special educator. Four years running an autism program in Iowa. Nearly a decade in the early childhood and autism space.

I primarily taught STAR, and I largely taught myself the curriculum. So when I joined STAR, I wasn't an outsider learning what we sell. I was a power user joining the company that built the thing I'd already been using to change kids' lives.

Here's the part that doesn't fit neatly on a resume: STAR is the reason I became an effective teacher. I watched my students grow in ways I could measure and feel, and it was this curriculum and this approach that got us there. My loyalty to this company isn't abstract. It's the thing that taught me how to do the work I love, and I want to help other educators find what I found.

still very much one of them.

I'm wired for outcomes and for people. I want the work to ship and I want it to actually serve the humans on the receiving end. I lead through structure and quiet conviction more than volume. That doesn't mean I don't have opinions. It means I want them earned through evidence and shared at the right moment.

The role on paper hasn't kept up with what the role actually is in practice. What started as customer-facing enablement has organically grown to include product and R&D collaboration, instructional design, marketing and messaging, Pendo and digital guidance, and systems work that touches nearly every department.

That's not scope creep. That's a role finding its real shape.

I need a creative element in the work to feel whole. I want to be in the making. That's why the work naturally pulls me toward design, instructional craft, visual systems, and storytelling, even when the job description doesn't strictly call for it. The creative side isn't a hobby sitting next to my "real" work. It's the thing that makes the rest of it actually worth doing.

Interlude

Where I see the work going

Eight directions I'd help shape, drive where I can, and partner on the rest.

vision ahead

Pendo as connective tissue

Making Pendo an everyday tool for Customer Success, trainers, and sales, and a real on-ramp for customers to find value from the first login. Plus a channel for customer insight that actually reaches product and R&D, and influences what gets built next.

A confident floor for customers without services

Helping curriculum-only customers find real value from day one, with scalable resources that meet them where they are. Services is where the deepest transformation happens and our crown jewel. This enablement is about giving everyone else a real starting line, protecting retention, and creating natural paths toward the partnership they haven't opted into yet.

Keeping valuable initiatives on track and on time

Shepherding the work that matters most, with educators, teachers, and the kids they serve as the measure of "done well." This is the project management instinct at work.

Case studies: video and written

Telling STAR's story through real classrooms, real districts, real outcomes. The proof that lives between the product and the people it actually serves.

A teacher creator program, made real

I'm on STAR's social media team, and we already do this informally with educators who use STAR. The vision: a real program with two layers. STAR champions showing how the work shows up in their classrooms every day, and strategic partnerships with established SPED creators who bring our story to broader audiences. Vetted for values and fit, with real perks, real terms, and real brand protection. Social that lands because it's coming from teachers, not us. With a way to keep our champions close while we lift each other up.

Microlearning & module content

Storyline, video-based learning, trainer-facing and CSS-facing content, written in the language that matters to each learner and the lives they come from. Working across mediums, leaning into video.

projects I'd love to drive: Student Learning Profile how-to Coach Certification course Behavior Mini-Series Paraprofessional Series

A Help experience that actually helps

Right now, there's no single front door for help. A personal message, a website form, the info inbox, the help desk that doesn't have the visibility it deserves. The vision: a centralized Help Desk where customers find real answers fast, support shows up at the point of need, and Pendo surfaces the right help when it detects friction. Quick and easy, no matter what tier. Less digging, more value from the first question.

A formal cross-functional role

The connector: across product, R&D, marketing, training, and customer success. Doing the translation work officially, not just in the margins.

The translation between what leadership imagines, what teams build, and what actually reaches a teacher at 7am. Invisible until it isn't. Essential either way.

Chapter Four

Three paths forward

I'm not looking for a different role. I'm looking to make the one I already have legible, and to grow it intentionally as STAR evolves. Here are three shapes that could work. Each one plays to strengths; each has tradeoffs. Pick the one that best serves STAR.

ch. 04 forward
Path 01
Senior IC, full cross-functional range

Customer Experience & Enablement Lead

Let the role become what it's becoming. Formalize the eight workstreams, elevate the title and seniority to reflect the real range, and make room for the creative and instructional work I'm already pulling toward.

  • Owns enablement, learning experience, and Pendo strategy end-to-end
  • Drives creative direction on microlearning and customer enablement content
  • Formal seat at cross-functional tables, with decision authority to match
  • Best for: recognizing the role that's already real, and letting it grow
Path 02
Current lane, plus a formal mandate

Customer Enablement & Special Projects Lead

A formal mandate to drive cross-functional initiatives that need someone to hold the thread. When strategic work is stuck between teams, when good ideas lose momentum after the meeting ends, when something needs to move — that's where this role lives. Not replacing functional owners. Accelerating them. Less "add another project to Bailey's list," more "give this work a name and a mandate."

  • Drives cross-functional initiatives that fall between org chart boxes
  • Holds threads and sequences next steps when work is losing momentum
  • Accelerates stuck work without replacing functional owners
  • Stays close to customers and educators, so strategy stays grounded in real classroom realities
  • Best for: formalizing the connective work already happening, without a structural overhaul
Path 03
Same work, made official

Senior Customer Enablement Specialist

Keep the current shape. Elevate the title to reflect seniority, adopt the RACI as a working agreement, and help leadership communicate the scope clearly across teams. Same work, more legible. Least disruptive, still high-impact.

  • Adopts the RACI above as a working agreement
  • Elevated title reflecting seniority and true scope
  • Leadership-backed communication so teams know where I plug in
  • Best for: stability, minimum change, maximum clarity
a personal note

I'm sharing this to make the work legible and to invite your thinking, not to pitch a title. What's right for me has to also be what's right for STAR, and that's a conversation I'd rather have with you.

That's the work. That's the lane.

What I'm asking for isn't a different role. It's clarity, visibility, and a conversation about which of these directions best serves STAR as it grows. And the chance to keep doing the work I love most: making complex things usable, helping leadership's ideas land, and making sure that what we build at STAR actually changes things for the educators and kids on the other end of it. And staying in conversation with them, whatever shape this role takes next.

I'm a translator. That's where I do my best work. And I'd love to make that official.
